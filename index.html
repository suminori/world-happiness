Are you happy with where you live?

The World Happiness Report is a landmark survey of the state of global happiness that ranks 156 countries by how happy their citizens perceive themselves to be. The World Happiness Report 2020 ranks cities around the world by their subjective well-being and digs more deeply into how the social, urban and natural environments combine to affect our happiness.

Why I chose this data

As a daughter to two fearless, hard-working immigrant parents, I am no stranger to the concept of relocating to another country in search of happiness and better opportunities than the ones you were born with. Now, as an upcoming graduate with the rest of my life ahead of me, I'm often pondering the question of what it means to be happy; and more specifically, what it means to be happy with the place you call home. When I came across this dataset, I was interested in seeing how the relative levels of happiness were determined, and how the two places where I have roots compare to each other in this regard.

About the dataset

Although the annual World Happiness Report is based on a wide variety of data, the most important source is the Gallup World Poll, an organization that collects data on global issues through telephone and face-to-face surveys of citizens. The Happiness Rankings are calculated based on a number of factors, which includes objective information like GDP per capita and life expectancy, as well as subjective binary responses to survey questions. For the purposes of this exploration, I decided to focus on the six subjective factors included in the report.

Freedom to Make Life Choices

The national average of binary (0 or 1) responses to the GWP question "Are you satisfied or dissatisfied with your freedom to choose what you do with your life?"

Perceptions of Corruption

The national average of binary responses to: "Is corruption widespread throughout the government or not" and "Is corruption widespread within businesses or not?"

Social Support

The national average of binary responses to the GWP question "If you were in trouble, do you have relatives or friends you can count on to help you whenever you need them, or not?"

Generosity

The residual of regressing national average of response to the GWP question "Have you donated money to a charity in the past month?" on GDP per capita.

Positive Affect

The national average of binary responses to questions of whether a respondent had felt happiness, laughter, and/or enjoyment during "a lot of" the previous day.

Negative Affect

The national average of binary responses to questions of whether a respondent had felt worry, sadness, and/or anger during "a lot of" the previous day.

Takeaways

  • The scatter plot was helpful in visualizing the landscape of data included in the report. From it, it is apparent that the happiest countries all seem to have high levels of individual freedom and social support, and higer trust in the integrity of their institutions.
  • From my comparison of my two home countries, it seems that in all the selected factors, the US wins out over Korea. However, as these values are derived from subjective responses, the results have only made me more curious as to why citizens of each country feel the way that they do, and what other factors may be directly related to these sentiments (quality of education, access to information, cultural values, etc).
